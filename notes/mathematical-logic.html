1. Propositional Logic

Propositional logic is the study of propositions and the logical connectives that combine them. A proposition is any statement that is either true or false.

Definition A well-formed formula (wff) is defined inductively: every propositional variable $p, q, r, \ldots$ is a wff; if $\varphi$ and $\psi$ are wffs, then so are $\neg\varphi$, $(\varphi \wedge \psi)$, $(\varphi \vee \psi)$, and $(\varphi \to \psi)$.

2. First-Order Logic

First-order logic (FOL) extends propositional logic with quantifiers $\forall$ and $\exists$, allowing us to reason about objects in a domain and their properties.

Theorem — Completeness (Gödel, 1930) A first-order sentence $\varphi$ is provable in FOL if and only if it is valid, i.e., $\vdash \varphi \iff \models \varphi$.

The completeness theorem guarantees that the syntactic and semantic notions of logical consequence coincide — every logical truth can be derived by proof.

3. Incompleteness

Gödel's incompleteness theorems establish fundamental limits on formal systems powerful enough to express basic arithmetic.

Theorem — First Incompleteness (Gödel, 1931) Any consistent formal system $F$ within which a certain amount of elementary arithmetic can be carried out is incomplete: there are statements of the language of $F$ which can neither be proved nor disproved in $F$.
